03 Consultation Framework

The 30-minute PT consultation

A structured conversation that uncovers needs, builds trust, and leads naturally to a recommendation.

1

Warm-Up & Rapport

5 Minutes

Start casual. Learn about them as a person before diving into fitness. This isn't small talk—it's intelligence gathering that helps you personalize your recommendation.

Questions to Ask
  • How long have you been a member here?"
  • What's your usual workout routine look like?"
  • What do you do for work? Active job or desk job?"
2

Goal Discovery

8 Minutes

Go deeper than surface goals. "Lose weight" isn't specific enough. Find the emotional driver—the wedding, the health scare, the desire to keep up with kids.

Questions to Ask
  • If we fast-forward 90 days, what does success look like for you?"
  • What made you decide now is the time to try training?"
  • What have you tried before? What worked, what didn't?"
  • On a scale of 1-10, how important is achieving this goal?"
3

Obstacle Mapping

5 Minutes

Understand what's gotten in the way before. These obstacles become the reasons why they need a trainer—accountability, knowledge, motivation.

Questions to Ask
  • What usually gets in the way of consistency for you?"
  • When you've fallen off track before, what happened?"
  • What would make this time different?"
4

The Recommendation

7 Minutes

Now you have everything you need. Connect their goals and obstacles to a specific trainer and package. Make it feel tailored, not scripted.

Framework
  • Based on [goal], I think [Trainer] would be perfect for you because..."
  • Given that [obstacle] has been challenging, I'd recommend [package] because..."
  • The investment is [price], which breaks down to [per session] per session."
5

Handle & Close

5 Minutes

Address concerns, answer questions, and ask for the commitment. Don't leave it open-ended—always ask for a decision or a clear next step.

Closing Questions
  • What questions do you have about getting started?"
  • Does Tuesday or Thursday work better for your first session?"
  • Should I get you scheduled with [Trainer] this week?"

05 Objection Handling

Turning hesitation into commitment

PT objections are different from membership objections. The stakes feel higher. Here's how to respond.

$ "It's too expensive."

This is the most common objection. Reframe the cost in terms of value and investment in themselves. Compare to other things they spend money on.

Try This "I hear you—it is an investment. Let me ask: what would it be worth to you to actually achieve [their goal]? When you break it down, the 12-session package is about $75 per session, which is less than a nice dinner out. Except this investment compounds—the knowledge and habits you build stay with you forever."
? "I can figure it out myself."

Don't argue—agree and expand. Yes, they could. But the question is whether they will, and how long it will take.

Try This "You absolutely could—there's tons of information out there. But here's what I've seen: most people who try to figure it out alone spend months or years doing things that don't work. A trainer compresses that learning curve. You get the right program from day one, proper form so you don't get injured, and someone who adjusts the plan as you progress. Is time something you have plenty of, or would you rather get results faster?"
⏱ "I don't have time."

Time is often a proxy for priority. Help them see that training actually saves time by making workouts more efficient.

Try This "I get it—you're busy. Here's the thing: most people who work out on their own spend time wandering between machines, figuring out what to do. With a trainer, every minute is purposeful. A 45-minute session with [Trainer] is more effective than 90 minutes on your own. Plus, the accountability means you actually show up. Would two 45-minute sessions per week be possible?"
😰 "I want to get in shape first."

This is fear disguised as logic. Gently point out the contradiction and normalize starting exactly where they are.

Try This "I hear that a lot, and I totally understand the instinct. But think about it: that's like saying 'I want to get healthy before I see a doctor.' The whole point of working with a trainer is to meet you where you are. [Trainer] has worked with people at every fitness level—including plenty who hadn't exercised in years. You don't need to prepare for training. Training IS the preparation."
🤔 "I need to think about it."

There's usually something specific behind this. Probe gently to find the real concern.

Try This "Of course—it's a decision worth making thoughtfully. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Sometimes when people say that, there's a question I haven't answered well. Is it the cost, the time commitment, or something about whether it'll actually work for you?"
06 Closing Techniques

Four ways to secure the commitment

Read the prospect and choose the approach that fits their personality.

The Choice Close

When they're interested but haven't chosen a package

"Based on your goals, I'd recommend either the 8-session Foundation or the 12-session Transformation. The 12 gives you better per-session value and more time to build habits. Which feels right for where you're at?"

The Calendar Close

When they're ready but need a push to start

"[Trainer] has availability Tuesday at 6pm and Thursday at 7am. Which works better for your first session? I'll get you on the calendar right now."

The Vision Close

When they need to reconnect with their motivation

"You told me [their goal] is really important to you. Imagine it's 90 days from now and you've been training consistently. How would that feel? What would be different? That's what we're signing up for today."

The Trial Close

When they're hesitant despite being a good fit

"Here's what I'd suggest: start with the 4-session Starter. Commit to one month with [Trainer]. If after four sessions you don't feel like it's worth it, no pressure to continue. But I think you'll be hooked."
07 Follow-Up Sequences

When they don't buy today

Most people won't commit on the first conversation. A good follow-up sequence keeps the door open.

Same Day

The Thank You Text

Personal, brief, no pressure. Just appreciation and an open door.

"Hey [Name], great meeting you today! Let me know if any questions come up about training. I'm here whenever you're ready. – [Your name]"
Day 3

The Value Add

Share something useful—not a sales pitch. Build trust and stay top of mind.

"Hey [Name], I was thinking about your [goal]. Here's a quick tip that might help even before you start training: [relevant tip]. Let me know how it goes!"
Day 7

The Check-In

Casual follow-up. Reference their specific situation.

"Hi [Name], just checking in. Have you had a chance to think about what we discussed? [Trainer] has some openings next week if you want to get started. No rush—just wanted to keep you posted."
Day 14

The Final Reach

Last active outreach. Give them an easy way to re-engage.

"Hey [Name], I'll keep this short—I know you're busy. If training is still on your radar, I'd love to help you get started. If timing isn't right, no worries at all. Just reply 'later' and I'll check back in a month, or 'ready' and I'll get you scheduled this week."
